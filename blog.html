The geometry of forgetting in continual learning

Companion note to the draft Interference and retention in continual learning. Draft PDF

When a network learns tasks in sequence it tends to suffer catastrophic forgetting: weights move to satisfy the new objective and, in doing so, dismantle the features that served the old one. The usual responses are remedial. Experience replay retrains on stored samples; elastic regularisation penalises motion in directions the Fisher information marks as important. Both work. Neither says what forgetting is.

The claim in this note is that forgetting is best read as geometric interference between task directions. Fix the features (the frozen-feature or linearised regime — a strong assumption, discussed below) and let Δ be the weight update produced by learning Task B. The loss increase on Task A is then exactly a quadratic form:

F(Δ) = ½ ΔT ΣA Δ

Here ΣA is the feature covariance of Task A. The reading is direct. Where ΣA has large eigenvalues, Task A's predictions depend sharply on those coordinates, and any component of Δ lying along them is expensive. Where ΣA has an exact null space, motion is free: forgetting is zero, not merely small. Everything in between is a matter of eigenvalue, not of intuition.

Where this breaks. The identity above holds when features are frozen and the loss is locally quadratic. Under end-to-end training the features themselves move, ΣA is no longer a constant, and F becomes a first-order approximation whose error grows with the size of the update. It is a lens, not a theorem about deep networks.

Interference-gated functional allocation (IGFA)

If forgetting is interference, the remedy is not a penalty but a projection. IGFA is a replay-free structural controller: it measures the alignment between a proposed update and the dominant directions of earlier tasks. Below a threshold τ the directions are effectively independent, the update passes untouched, and capacity is shared. Above it, IGFA projects the update onto the orthogonal complement of the protected subspace, keeping the descent direction it can afford and discarding the rest.

Two consequences are worth stating plainly, because they are the cost of the method:

  • The projection discards part of the gradient. Task B learns more slowly, and in the limit of many protected tasks the free subspace shrinks toward nothing — a capacity ceiling, not a free lunch.
  • The gate is a hard threshold, so behaviour is discontinuous at τ. Whether that matters depends on how alignment is distributed in practice; on the sequences tested it does not, but that is an empirical claim about those sequences.

Solving the electrode calendering U-shape puzzle

Companion note to the draft Capturing the calendering U-shape in lithium-ion electrode thermal conductivity. Draft PDF

Lithium-ion cells depend on calendered electrodes for volumetric energy density. Predicting what compression does to through-plane thermal conductivity (λeff) turns out to be harder than it looks. The intuition is monotone and wrong: press the particles together, porosity falls, gas-filled gaps disappear, heat should flow more easily.

Measurements disagree. Through-plane conductivity drops through the early stages of calendering and only recovers at high compaction — a U-shape. Porosity-only closures cannot produce a minimum at all; static contact models rise too slowly but still rise. Something is being removed as the electrode densifies.

The physics of particle reorientation

The mechanism is the anisotropy of graphite. Conductivity within the graphene planes is high; perpendicular to them it is poor, by more than an order of magnitude. Compression does not merely bring particles closer. It reorients them, laying the flakes flat against the current collector.

Flat flakes present their poor through-plane axis to the direction heat must travel. Early calendering therefore trades a shrinking pore volume for a worsening particle orientation, and orientation wins. Only once the flakes are largely aligned — when reorientation has little left to give — does the growth of particle-to-particle contact area take over, and the curve turns upward.

The draft captures this with a calendering-aware Zehner–Bauer–Schlünder closure: a compression-indexed contact term φ(Π) on a Knudsen-corrected ZBS base, with the orientation effect entering as a bounded correction rather than a free-floating fit parameter. Across 27 calendering states the mean absolute percentage error falls from 31.1% to 4.5%.

What that number is and is not. 4.5% MAPE is in-sample across 27 states of one anode chemistry. It shows the closure can represent the U-shape; it does not establish that it transfers to other formulations, cathodes, or coating thicknesses. Held-out validation across chemistries is the obvious next test, and the honest reason to call this a draft.

What the slider makes visible is that the minimum is not a fitting artefact. It is where two monotone processes — falling porosity and worsening orientation — cross in their influence on the same transport path. Any closure that carries only one of them is forced to be monotone, and will be wrong on one side of the minimum or the other.
