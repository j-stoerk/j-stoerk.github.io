The Geometry of Forgetting in Continual Learning

How does learning a new task damage old ones? By analyzing weight updates through the lens of feature covariance, we model forgetting as interference energy and introduce a replay-free structural controller: Interference-Gated Functional Allocation (IGFA).

When a neural network learns new tasks sequentially, it often suffers from catastrophic forgetting: the weights shift to optimize the new objective, erasing the features learned for prior tasks. Most standard mitigations rely on post-hoc remedies like experience replay (re-training on stored past samples) or elastic regularization (penalizing changes to important weights via Fisher information).

In this work, we argue that forgetting should instead be modeled directly as geometric interference between task directions. In the frozen-feature regime, the forgetting of Task A when learning Task B is exactly the interference energy:

F = ½ ΔT ΣA Δ

where ΣA is the feature covariance matrix of Task A, and Δ is the weight update vector from learning Task B. If the update vector Δ overlaps with directions where Task A has high variance, it will degrade Task A's performance. Conversely, if Δ lies in the null space of ΣA, forgetting is exactly zero.

Interference-Gated Functional Allocation (IGFA)

From this geometric insight, we derive IGFA, a replay-free structural controller. IGFA monitors the alignment between new task updates and past task workspaces. When task directions align cooperatively, IGFA permits parameter sharing. When they conflict, IGFA projects the proposed update onto the orthogonal complement of the old task's workspace, protecting the critical directions from distortion.

Solving the Electrode Calendering U-Shape Puzzle

Porosity-only thermal models predict a monotonic rise in conductivity, failing to explain real-world U-shaped behavior. We show how a calendering-aware Zehner–Bauer–Schlünder closure explains this anomaly.

Lithium-ion batteries rely on calendered (compressed) electrodes to achieve high volumetric energy density. However, predicting how compression affects through-plane thermal conductivity (λeff) is notoriously difficult. Historically, models assumed a monotonic increase in conductivity with density: as particles are pressed closer, porosity decreases, which should always improve thermal pathways.

Yet, experimental measurements reveal a baffling U-shape curve: during initial calendering, through-plane conductivity actually drops before rising again at high compaction. Porosity-only and static-contact closures fail to explain this drop.

The Physics of Particle Reorientation

The secret lies in the anisotropy of graphite particles. Graphite has extremely high thermal conductivity in-plane, but poor conductivity through-plane (perpendicular to the graphene layers). As the anode is compressed, the active material particles do not just get closer; they undergo reorientation, laying flat parallel to the current collector.

This horizontal alignment acts as a thermal barrier, decreasing through-plane conductivity. Only under extreme compression does the growth of contact area between particles finally dominate, restoring vertical heat flow and generating the U-shape. We capture this with a calendering-aware Zehner–Bauer–Schlünder (ZBS) closure by introducing a compression-indexed contact term φ(Π) on a Knudsen-corrected ZBS base, reducing the Mean Absolute Percentage Error across 27 calendering states from 31.1% to 4.5%.
